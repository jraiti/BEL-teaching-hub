Overview & Rationale

What IOAs are and why they matter

IOAs help students articulate reasoning, integrate knowledge, and demonstrate application in context. Assessors use open‚Äëended prompts to probe understanding and adapt to student responses, supporting validity and authenticity while also addressing integrity risks.

Designing IOAs

Align to outcomes, criteria, and evidence of learning

Identify outcomes and criteria
  • Identify which course learning outcomes the IOA will assess (typically reasoning, application/synthesis, communication).
  • Draft assessment criteria aligned to the outcomes and the IOA‚Äôs authentic context (e.g., client briefing, viva, design review).
  • Where defending a pre‚Äësubmitted artefact, mark the artefact first and prepare questions based on it.
Format & logistics

Choose a format consistent with disciplinary norms and integrity aims:

  • Interactive simulation, scenario role‚Äëplay, viva voce, in‚Äëperson interview, or defence of an artefact.
  • Schedule across the cohort (may span multiple days); design open‚Äëended prompts to limit advantages from prior sharing.
  • Recordings or co‚Äëassessment by two markers improve reliability for performance/presentation components.
Fairness & inclusion
  • Publish criteria/standards early (by end of Week 1) and use student‚Äëfriendly descriptions of tasks/requirements.
  • Use varied prompt types to elicit evidence from diverse communicative styles; avoid leading or overly probing questions that threaten validity.
  • Permit extensions and reasonable adjustments via school/faculty processes where appropriate.

Semi‚ÄëStructured Questions

Purposeful prompts that evidence the criteria

 IOAs are designed to be nuanced and interactive. They are dynamic and personalised for free-flowing conversation format. This a series of semi-structured questions and follow up prompts which allow the students to respond and demonstrate how they meet rubric criteria.

Use a small number of main questions aligned to each criterion, with flexible follow‚Äëups to probe reasoning, accuracy, and application. Avoid closed and leading questions that narrow responses or bias judgements.

üéØ Principles

Principles that aid in semi-structured questions: 

  • Formulate an overall aim for the IOA.
  • Use open-ended questions to keep the conversation flowing and allow the student to provide a detailed response. These questions could ask for concrete or narrative information.
  • Formulate a main question for each assessment criterion. This alignment in the rubric can follow a progressive order if the direction of the IOA is known, and will aid in marking.
  • Use follow-up questions and prompts (both pre-designed and spontaneous) under each main question to elucidate the fullest response from the student for that criterion. Follow-up questions/prompts can be used to:
    • Contribute to fluency of the conversation
    • Clarify/expand on response to the criteria
    • Gain accurate information
    • Introduce new information or a counterargument for further testing
    • Redirect the conversation back to criteria
  • Avoid leading questions. These can bias students‚Äô responses and threaten validity of assessment.
  • Avoid probing questions to establish how well the student understands a specific area of knowledge as this may threaten validity of assessment.
  • Avoid closed questions: this impedes the flow of conversation and interactivity that is core to an interactive oral assessment piece.
